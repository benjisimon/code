  ---                                                                                   
  The "Curse of Ham" in Traditional Jewish Sources                                      
                                                                                        
  The Biblical Text                                                                     
                                                                                        
  Genesis 9:20–27 (https://www.sefaria.org/Genesis.9.20-27):

  "Cursed be Canaan; the lowest of slaves shall he be to his brothers... Blessed be the
  Eternal, the God of Shem; let Canaan be a slave to them. May God enlarge Japheth...
  and let Canaan be a slave to them."

  The Civil War pro-slavery argument ran: Ham sinned → Ham's descendants (including
  Africans) are cursed to be slaves. But traditional Jewish commentators saw this text
  very differently, and the argument starts falling apart immediately.

  ---
  The Rabbinic Sources

  1. Rashi on Genesis 9:25 (11th c., France) —
  https://www.sefaria.org/Rashi_on_Genesis.9.25

  "You have brought it about that I cannot beget a fourth son to serve me; cursed,
  therefore, be your fourth son to serve under the descendants of these elder ones."

  Rashi explains the curse mechanically: Ham (by castrating or assaulting Noah, per
  Sanhedrin 70a) prevented Noah from having a fourth son — so Noah cursed Ham's fourth
  son. The "fourth son" of Ham is Canaan, not Cush. Rashi treats this as a very
  specific, bounded curse with a specific cause.

  2. Sanhedrin 70a — https://www.sefaria.org/Sanhedrin.70a

  The Talmud actually debates what Ham's crime was:

  "Rav and Shmuel disagreed: One says that Ham castrated Noah and one says that Ham
  sodomized him... This Sage holds that both this offense and that offense were
  committed."

  The rabbis understood Ham's sin as a violent sexual crime, and Noah's curse as a
  proportional legal response — not a sweeping racial decree about all Africans forever.

  3. Sanhedrin 108b — https://www.sefaria.org/Sanhedrin.108b

  This is the one passage that does link Ham to dark skin:

  "Three violated that directive [against intercourse in the ark]... Ham, son of Noah.
  The dog was punished in that it is bound; the raven was punished in that it spits, and
   Ham was afflicted in that his skin turned black."

  This is the Talmudic source for associating Ham with dark skin — but notice: it's
  framed as a personal punishment for sexual sin during the ark, not as a divinely
  ordained racial destiny for all his descendants. And the text says nothing about
  slavery here — that falls on Canaan specifically.

  4. Bereishit Rabbah 36 (Midrash, ~500 CE) —
  https://www.sefaria.org/Bereishit_Rabbah.36

  The Midrash elaborates, with Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba saying:

  "Both Ham and the dog engaged in sexual relations in the ark. That is why Ham emerged
  darkened, and the dog is exposed when mating."

  And, linking the darkening to Canaan:

  "Rav Huna said in the name of Rav Yosef: 'You prevented me from performing an act that
   is done in darkness; therefore, that man [Canaan] will be ugly and darkened.'"

  So the Midrash does connect Ham to dark skin and Canaan to being "darkened." But the
  text immediately addresses the obvious puzzle...

  5. Bereishit Rabbah 36 — The Puzzle the Rabbis Themselves Ask

  *"Ham committed the sin and Canaan was cursed? This is astonishing."*

  The Midrash poses this problem directly. Why is Canaan cursed for Ham's act? The
  rabbis give several answers (Canaan saw and told Ham; Ham couldn't be cursed directly
  because God had blessed Noah's sons). The point is: the rabbis knew this was not a
  simple racial decree, and spent considerable effort explaining its logic.

  6. Ibn Ezra on Genesis 9:25 (12th c., Spain) —
  https://www.sefaria.org/Ibn_Ezra_on_Genesis.9.25.2

  This is the most direct refutation — written 700 years before the Civil War:

  *"There are some who say that the Cushites are enslaved because Noah cursed Ham.
  However, they have forgotten that the first king to rule after the flood was a
  Cushite. Thus it is written, 'and Cush begot Nimrod… and the beginning of his kingdom
  was Babel, and Erech, and Accad, and Calneh, in the land of Shinar' (Gen. 10:8–10).
  Thus the curse was put on the Canaanites and not on all of Ham's descendants."*

  Ibn Ezra is explicitly arguing against the same reading Americans would use in 1861 —
  and his refutation is decisive on its own terms.

  7. Sforno on Genesis 9:25 (16th c., Italy) —
  https://www.sefaria.org/Sforno_on_Genesis.9.25

  "His being in a position of dependence to his senior brothers, i.e. a plain slave, was
   merely natural, and would not have been a special curse, especially in view of his
  disgraceful conduct. Solomon paraphrased this in Proverbs 11:29: 'a fool is a slave to
   the wise-hearted.'"

  Sforno spiritualizes the curse almost entirely — it's about moral degradation, not
  racial destiny.

  ---
  Would the American Argument Make Sense in a Traditional Jewish Context?

  No — and for multiple compounding reasons:

  1. Wrong person. The curse falls on Canaan, Ham's fourth son — ancestor of the
  Canaanites, not of Africans. Cush (Ethiopia/Africa) is a different son entirely, and
  Ibn Ezra says explicitly that Cush is not covered by the curse.

  2. Nimrod demolishes it. Cush's grandson Nimrod was the first king after the flood —
  builder of Babylon, Nineveh, an empire. The idea that Cush was destined for slavery is
   directly contradicted by the very next chapter of Genesis.

  3. The curse is already fulfilled. Traditional Jewish interpretation understood the
   become "hewers of wood and drawers of water" — that's the realization the rabbis
   become "hewers of wood and drawers of water" — that's the realization the rabbis
  pointed to. It wasn't an open-ended mandate for all time.

  4. Ham's dark skin is about sin, not race. The Talmudic and Midrashic passages that do
   link Ham to dark skin frame it as punishment for sexual sin in the ark — not as a
  permanent racial hierarchy. And they apply specifically to Ham, not to all his
  descendants.

  5. The rabbis themselves called the logic "astonishing." The Midrash openly flags that
   cursing Canaan for Ham's act is a puzzle requiring explanation. A traditional reader
  would know this wasn't a simple divine decree about African peoples.

  6. Jewish law on slavery is nothing like American chattel slavery. The Torah requires
  freeing Hebrew slaves after 7 years; even Canaanite slaves could earn freedom if their
   master struck out a tooth (Exodus 21:27). The permanent, hereditary, race-based
  chattel slavery of the antebellum South had no parallel in halakhic slave law.

  7. The Passover irony. The entire theological framework of Jewish life rests on the
  Exodus — God caring about the suffering of slaves and liberating them. "You shall not
  oppress the stranger because you were strangers in Egypt" (Exodus 22:20) appears 36
  times in the Torah. A rabbi making this argument at a Seder would be doing so in front
   of a text whose whole point is that enslaving people is what Pharaoh does.

  ---
  The One Jewish Voice Who Made the Argument Anyway

  Rabbi Morris Raphall (New York) gave a famous pro-slavery sermon on January 4, 1861 —
  "Bible View of Slavery" — which used exactly this Canaan/Ham argument. He was the only
   major American rabbi to do so. He was immediately attacked by Rabbi David Einhorn
  (Baltimore), who pointed out that the curse was on Canaan specifically, that biblical
  slavery was unrecognizable as chattel slavery, and that Raphall's argument required
  ignoring most of what the tradition actually said. M. Heilprin's blistering Tribune
  letter (already in your research notes) was also a direct response to Raphall.

  The pro-slavery reading of Genesis 9 was, in the traditional Jewish context,
  essentially a misreading that ignored the specific identity of "Canaan," the
  already-fulfilled prophecy, the limits of biblical slave law, and the central
  theological weight of the Exodus story itself.

  The comparison between them is interesting: Stringfellow is a Virginia Baptist
  minister making a blunt case for slaveholder piety. Raphall is a New York rabbi making
   a more sophisticated textual argument — he uses the Hebrew word eved, he's aware of
  the Rationalist objection about the curse being invented to justify the Canaanite
  conquest, and he frames it as one of three eternal racial prophecies. But he makes
  exactly the same core error: he slides from "Canaan" to "all of Ham's descendants" to
  "the African race" in three sentences, and never once wrestles with the fact that Cush
   (the African branch) was explicitly excluded from the curse by Ibn Ezra 700 years
  earlier. Raphall, as a rabbi, had no excuse for not knowing that.
